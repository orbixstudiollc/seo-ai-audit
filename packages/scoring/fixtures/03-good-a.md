# A Practical Guide to Writing Content Briefs

A good content brief tells a writer exactly what to write without telling them how to write it. It should fit on one page and answer four questions: who this is for, what the reader should do after reading, what "done" looks like, and what must not be missed. Skip any one of the four and the first draft will miss the mark.

## Who Is the Piece Actually For?

Naming an audience segment isn't enough; a brief needs to describe what that reader already knows and what they're trying to decide. A brief that says "marketers" gives a writer nothing to work with. A brief that says "a marketing manager who already tried three tools and is comparing a fourth" gives the writer an actual person to write to.

Include what the reader is likely skeptical about, too. If they've been burned by vague claims before, the writer needs to know to lead with specifics instead of adjectives.

## What Should the Reader Do After Reading?

Every brief needs one clear next action, not three vague ones. Sign up for a trial, book a call, or bookmark the piece for later reference are three completely different outcomes, and writing toward all three at once produces a draft that pushes none of them well.

Pick the single action that matters most for this specific piece and let every section build toward it.

## What Does "Done" Look Like?

This is the section most briefs skip, and it's the reason revisions drag on. A finished brief should describe the shape of the finished piece: how many sections, roughly how long, what tone, and what it should feel like to read the last paragraph.

A few things worth spelling out before the writer starts:

- The rough word count range, not just a single target number
- Whether the piece needs a personal example or can stay general
- The tone: is this piece allowed to have an opinion, or should it stay neutral

## What Must Not Be Missed?

Every topic has a landmine: a competitor claim that can't be repeated, a legal line that can't be crossed, or a past piece the new one shouldn't contradict. Writers who don't know about these land in revision loops that have nothing to do with writing quality.

## Putting the Brief Together

A brief that answers all four questions on one page will save more editing time than any style guide. The goal isn't a longer document; it's a document a writer can read once and start from immediately, without a clarifying call first.
