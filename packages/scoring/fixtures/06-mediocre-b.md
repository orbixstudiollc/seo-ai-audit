# Content Repurposing Strategies for Small Teams

## Introduction

Small marketing teams are often stretched thin, juggling a dozen different responsibilities with a fraction of the headcount that bigger companies have available to throw at the same problems, and one of the ways smart teams try to close that gap is by getting more mileage out of content they've already created instead of constantly starting from scratch every single time a new piece is needed for the calendar. This general idea, usually called content repurposing, has become a popular topic in marketing circles over the past several years, and there's a reason it keeps coming up in conversations among people who run lean teams and don't have the luxury of a large content department to lean on whenever something new needs to get made.

## Overview of the Approach

The basic approach involves taking one piece of long-form content, like a blog post or a webinar recording, and breaking it down into smaller pieces that can be published across different channels over time, which stretches the value of the original research and writing effort across weeks or even months instead of getting used once and then forgotten about entirely the way so much content ends up being treated by teams that are moving too fast to think about reuse. It also means the team spends more of its limited time on the harder work of original research and less time on reformatting, although reformatting still takes real effort that shouldn't be underestimated when planning out a repurposing calendar for the weeks ahead.

## Main Points to Keep in Mind

There are a handful of things worth keeping in mind when building out a repurposing habit on a small team, and the biggest one is probably sequencing, since publishing every derived piece on the same day defeats the purpose of stretching the content's lifespan across a longer window of time. It also helps to think about which channels actually reach the audience segments who haven't already seen the original piece, rather than just cross-posting the same message everywhere out of habit without considering whether it's reaching anyone new at all, which is a mistake that happens more often than most teams would like to admit when they're moving quickly and not stopping to think it through carefully enough before hitting publish on the next derived piece in the queue. On top of that, nobody on the team ever seems to circle back and check whether any of it actually moved a metric that mattered in the first place, which is its own separate problem worth thinking about on a different day.

## Conclusion

Repurposing works best as a habit built into the planning process from the start, not an afterthought applied to old content after the fact once someone remembers it exists sitting in the archive somewhere.
